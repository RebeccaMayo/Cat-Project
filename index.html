The Cats of 64th Street Park
Miami Beach, Florida

Welcome! This website was created to educate the world about the colony of cats that lives in and around the boardwalk at 64th Street Park in Miami Beach, Florida. Here you will learn all about these wonderful cats and the team of locals that love and care for them.

Meet the Cats

The Cat Gallery

During the day you would be lucky to see one or two cats near the boardwalk, but as the sun begins to set the cats become more active and come out from hiding. Some of the cats were born at the beach and have had little human contact. Others were feral but opened their hearts to human affection once shown warmth and kindness (and food). Others were abandoned here illegally by careless owners. Here are a few of the regulars that can be spotted in the park every evening. Each one of them has a unique personality and story.

About Us

A group of locals have come together to feed and care for the needs of the community cats. The cats are fed every night, rain or shine. We spend our own money food and medical care and do not receive support from the city.

Why are there so many cats here?

Cats were first brought to Miami Beach in 1912 by the city's earliest developers in an effort to reduce the rat population. You will not see any rats near the beach today, but the decendents of those first cats still claim the territory. The cats in this area have no natural predetors and they can easily survive the temperate winters. The population went unchecked for many years and grew to a large number. Today, there is a humane solution to population control.

Trap, Neuter, Release

You may notice that most of the cats are missing the very tip of their left ears. This notch signifies that the cat has been trapped, neutered, and released back to it's original home on the beach. These cats will not be able to reproduce, have been vaccinated, dewormed, and given flea treatment. They are often less aggressive to their fellow cats and friendlier to humans. The notch is taken from the ear while the cat is under anethesia during their spay or neuter surgery and the cat does not feel pain from the procedure. We work with local organizations to ensure that as many cats as possible got through the TNR program brfore they are able to have more kittens.'

Neonatal kittens

Occasionally, very young kittens are found abandoned. These kittens are too small to fend for themselves and need to be bottle fed. The kittens are left behind for various reasons. Sometimes the mother is not healthy enough to support the kitten, sometimes they become scared while moving them and the kitten is dropped. These little ones need special attention.
